Phillis Wheatley (1753-1784) First ever African woman to be published in Britain and America in 1773 Born in West Africa; sold as a slave to an AMerican family Moved to England age 20 Her first volume of poetry was published Her work was used as evidence that enslaved people were more than capable of intellectual originality and had a monumental influence on the anti-slavery movement Mary Seacole (1805-1881) Born in Jamaica; moved to England 1854 Crimean War → War Office refused to send her to the front as an army nurse; found her own way to raise funds and travel to Crimea Set up the British Hotel - nursed British soldiers back to health Overshadowed by Florence Nightingale 1991 campaign to acknowledge her work 1857: published ‘Wonderful Adventures of Mrs. Seacole in Many Lands’, the first known autobiography by a Black British Woman. https://www.bustle.com/life/black-british-firsts-a-timeline Alice Kinloch (1852-1915) Born in South Africa; came to Britain in 1895 Co-founded the African Association Worked to educate and mobilise people in the fight against colonialism and imperialism in Africa Convened the Pan-African Conference, 1900 Key figure in the Pan-African movement Amy Ashwood Garvey (1897-1969) Born in Jamaica Wife of Marcus Garvey Co-founded the Universal Negro Improvement Association Shaped the Garveyite movement 1924 came to London after divorcing Marcus Desire to help Black women “find themselves in life” Founded the Afro-Women’s Centre Opened the Afro-Woman Service Bureau at Egerton Gardens, London Co-founded the Nigerian Progress Union with Oladipo Solanke Created spaces for Black people to congregate in London - e.g. her International Afro Restaurant and the Florence Mills Social Club Key organiser 1945 Fifth Pan-African Congress in Manc Chaired the first session on freedom from British colonial rule Worked with Claudia Jones Claudia Jones (1915-1964) Born in Trinidad Moved to US as a child; member of CPUSA - deported in 1955 under McCarthyism and the McCarran Act. 1951: arrested/ charge with ‘un-american activities’ and imprisoned for a year. 1955 moved to Britain Communist; Marxist-Leninist; Pan-Africanist; feminist Founded the West Indian and Afro-Asian Gazette in 1958 Amy Ashwood = on the editorial board Involvement in the All African Women’s Freedom Movement 1959 Carnival - precursor to Notting Hill Included the Carnival Queen beauty contest Participated in hunger strike outside the South African embassy to protest Mandela’s incarceration Led a British Freedom March to the US embassy in solidarity with the US march in 1963 Connie Mark (1923-2007) 1943 joined the British Army Served in JA as part of Auxiliary Territorial Service for 10 years Moved to Britain 1954 Became a “trailblazer” in the British Black community → advocated for the significant role of women in the war Chari of the Friends of Mary SEacole Organisation Member of the West Indian Ex-Servicemen and Women’s Association Dame Jocelyn Barrow (1929-2020) Born in Trinidad Founding member and general secretary of Campaign Against Racial Discrimination (CARD) CARD lobbied for race relations legislation Responsible for the Race Relations Act 1968 Leading member of the North London West Indian Association 1968 vice-chair of the International Human Rights Year Committee Teacher at Furzedown Teachers College and trainer at the Institute of Education→ pioneered the introduction of multicultural education, stressing the needs of various ethinc groups in the UK 1984 co-founded Arawidi Publications - children’s publishing house Published children’s books in a variety of language forms inc West Indian dialects and Glaswegian 1981-88 governor of the BBC - first black woman to have been appointed to the board https://www.blackhistorymonth.org.uk/article/section/african-history/dame-jocelyn-barrow-more-needs-to-be-done-on-her-lifetime-work-on-racial-and-multi-cultural-awareness/ “I also believe that it’s part of our own responsibility as communities, as parents, as elders in our communities to ensure we learn our own history.” Dame Jocelyn Barrow (15 April 1929 – 9 April 2020), educationalist, racial equality, human rights and community activist.” Gerlin Bean (b.?) Born in Jamaica Attended the UK’s first National Women’s Liberation Conference in Oxford 1970 One of only two Black women present out of over 600 women Set about forming a women’s group within the Black Unity and Freedom Party (Marxist-Leninist) Went on to establish similar women’s chapters in other sections of London’s Black Power network 1973 created the Black Women’s Group
